Industry 4.0

Landing Portal  ·  History of the Industrial Revolutions

A brief history · four revolutions · one continuing arc

From the steam engine to the cyber-physical system

Every industrial revolution has been a collision between a new source of power and a new way of organising work. Water and steam gave us the factory. Electricity and the assembly line gave us mass production. Electronics and computing gave us automation. And now networked sensors, cloud platforms, and learning algorithms are giving us factories that adapt and negotiate with themselves — Industry 4.0.

Timeline
1.0 Mechanisation ~1760 – 1840

Steam, water, and the birth of the factory

The first industrial revolution began in Britain in the late 18th century. James Watt's improved steam engine (patented 1769), Richard Arkwright's water-powered cotton mill (Cromford, 1771), and Edmund Cartwright's power loom (1785) shifted production out of artisan workshops and into centralised factories. Cities swelled, rail networks knitted the country together, and for the first time human output was decoupled from muscle and daylight.

2.0 Mass production ~1870 – 1914

Electricity, the assembly line, and standardised interchangeable parts

The second revolution wired the world. Thomas Edison and Nikola Tesla brought affordable electric light and long-distance AC transmission; Frederick Winslow Taylor codified "scientific management"; and in 1913 Henry Ford installed the moving assembly line at the Highland Park plant, cutting Model T chassis assembly time from 12.5 hours to about 90 minutes. Steel from Bessemer converters, synthetic dyes from German chemistry, and the internal combustion engine transformed the material base of the economy.

"I will build a motor car for the great multitude." — Henry Ford, announcing the Model T, 1908

3.0 Digital automation ~1960 – 2000

Semiconductors, PLCs, and the programmable factory

Industry 3.0 is the revolution of the microchip. Jack Kilby's integrated circuit (1958), Dick Morley's Modicon 084 — the world's first programmable logic controller (1968) — and the rise of industrial robots (GM's Unimate, 1961) replaced hard-wired relay logic with software you could re-flash overnight. CAD/CAM closed the loop from drawing to milling machine. By 2000, a modern automotive plant ran on thousands of PLCs, SCADA dashboards, and six-axis robots, all coordinated by ethernet and MES software.

4.0 Cyber-physical systems 2011 – present

Networked sensors, cloud, AI, and the self-observing factory

The term Industrie 4.0 was introduced at the 2011 Hannover Fair by a German working group chaired by Henning Kagermann, Wolfgang Wahlster, and Wolf-Dieter Lukas, and was formalised in the 2013 report Recommendations for implementing the strategic initiative Industrie 4.0. The vision: merge the physical factory with its digital shadow so that every machine, part, and process is addressable, observable, and capable of negotiating its own role in production.

Where 3.0 gave us a factory you could program, 4.0 gives us a factory that reprograms itself — based on real-time sensor data, supply-chain signals, and machine-learning models trained on its own history.

"We are on the threshold of a new age where manufacturing systems become autonomous, self-organising, and aware of their environment." — Henning Kagermann et al., acatech / Industrie 4.0 Working Group, 2013
The nine pillars of Industry 4.0

Boston Consulting Group's 2015 taxonomy — still the most widely cited framing of what changed in the transition from 3.0 to 4.0.

1. Industrial IoT

Cheap networked sensors turn every machine into a data source; every part becomes addressable.

2. Big data & analytics

Pipelines ingest terabytes of telemetry per shift to drive quality, throughput, and predictive maintenance.

3. Cloud computing

Elastic compute for MES, simulation, and training ML models — no on-prem data-center required.

4. Cybersecurity

OT networks merge with IT; zero-trust, segmentation, and signed firmware become table stakes.

5. Simulation / digital twins

A live software replica of each machine, line, or plant runs in parallel, fed by real sensors.

6. Additive manufacturing

3D printing moves from prototype to production parts; supply chains become data, not logistics.

7. Autonomous robots

Collaborative cobots work alongside humans; AMRs move material without fixed paths.

8. Augmented reality

Operators see overlays of instructions, torque values, and fault diagnoses projected on the work piece.

9. System integration

Horizontal (supplier ↔ factory ↔ customer) and vertical (sensor ↔ ERP) data flows in one fabric.

Beyond 4.0 — Industry 5.0

In January 2021 the European Commission published Industry 5.0 — Towards a sustainable, human-centric and resilient European industry. Where 4.0 optimised for throughput and flexibility, 5.0 adds three explicit goals: human-centric (technology serves workers, not the other way around), sustainable (circular economy, net-zero production), and resilient (supply chains that survive pandemics, embargoes, and climate shocks).

Industry 5.0 doesn't replace 4.0 — it is a normative layer on top of it. The sensors, cloud, and AI of 4.0 are the substrate; the goals shift from "make more, faster" to "make it well, fairly, and forever."
